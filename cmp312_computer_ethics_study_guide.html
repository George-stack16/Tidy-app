1.1

Why ethics matters in code

Engineering optimizes for a single variable (latency, throughput, engagement). Philosophy teaches this is exactly how ethical catastrophes happen — you need multi-variable thinking for code's impact on human lives.

1.2 — Utilitarianism

The calculus of consequences

Bentham & Mill. Most ethical choice = greatest aggregate pleasure, least pain. Purely quantitative ("Engineer's Philosophy").

  • Hedonic Calculus: notification algorithm raises engagement but causes anxiety — does the sum stay positive?
  • Problem of the Minority: if 99% benefit but 1% are systematically harmed, Utilitarianism struggles to protect that 1%.
Like button: 1B people +1% connection vs 10M people severe anxiety. Does the sum stay positive?
1.3 — Deontology

The code of duty

Kant. Some actions are inherently right/wrong regardless of outcome. Categorical Imperative: "Act only according to that maxim you can will to become a universal law."

  • Universal Test: if every developer used Dark Patterns, would the internet still function? If no → unethical.
  • Users as ends, not means: every user has an inherent right to autonomy and truth.
1.4 — Virtue ethics

The excellence of the craft

Aristotle. Focuses on the character of the practitioner. "What would a virtuous engineer do?"

  • Golden Mean: virtue lies between two vices. Prudence in security = between Paranoia and Negligence.
  • Phronesis (Practical Wisdom): the "Senior Engineer" trait — instinctively finding the ethical snag in messy projects.
1.5–1.6 — Social Contract & Determinism

The digital agreement & SCOT

Hobbes/Locke/Rousseau: people give up freedoms for social benefits. ToS = digital social contract — only valid with mutual benefit and informed consent.

Hard Determinism

Technology follows its own path. Privacy's end was inevitable once the internet existed.

SCOT (counter)

Human choices — laws, design decisions, defaults — shape technology. Your API's defaults are an ethical act.

2.1–2.3

Social power & ACM/IEEE codes

Engineers hold "Esoteric Knowledge" the public cannot verify. If you write an algorithm for credit scores or bail eligibility, you are effectively acting as a judge.

  • Paramount rule (ACM & IEEE): the public interest comes first. If profit conflicts with public safety, side with the public.
  • Avoid harm, be honest, respect privacy, honor confidentiality (Whistleblower's Balance).
  • Claiming an 80%-accurate AI is "foolproof" = ethics violation.

Case study — Therac-25

The radiation machine that killed patients

A race condition in software caused lethal overdoses. Engineers ignored "Malfunction 54" errors because they believed the software was perfect.

Lesson: trusting your code more than human operators reporting errors = fundamental breach of the professional code. Lacked Humility and Rigorous Testing.
2.7 — Whistleblowing (DeGeorge 1981)

The 4-step protocol

1

Assess severity

Is the harm serious & considerable? Annoying algorithm ≠ whistleblowing. Algorithm encouraging self-harm in minors = whistleblowing.

2

Exhaust internal channels

Report to supervisor first. Keep a private dated log. Move to CTO/Board if ignored. Prove you were a "loyal employee" first.

3

Evidence threshold

Must have documented proof convincing to a reasonable, impartial observer. Warning: data exfiltration can hurt your legal standing.

4

Probability of success

You're not required to sacrifice your career for a lost cause. If going public won't prevent the harm, consider internal reform or resignation.

3.1–3.2

Privacy as a moral right

  • Privacy ≠ Security. Security is a technical state; privacy is a moral right.
  • Chilling Effect: being watched makes you subconsciously conform — suppressing dissent and innovation.
  • Contextual Integrity (Nissenbaum): privacy is violated when data moves between contexts (fitness data → insurer).
  • "Nothing to Hide" Fallacy: privacy is the right to define your own identity, not about hiding crime.
  • Digital Panopticon: internet users can't know if they're being watched → they self-censor.
3.3

Surveillance capitalism (Shoshana Zuboff)

Human experience is used as free raw material for hidden commercial practices of prediction and sales.

  • Behavioral Surplus: data collected beyond product improvement is used to predict future behavior.
  • Ethics of Prediction: using typing speed to detect depression and sell "comfort food" ads — service or violation?
3.4 — Privacy by Design (Ann Cavoukian)

7 foundational principles

  • Proactive, not reactive — anticipate privacy risks before they occur
  • Privacy as the default — opt-in to tracking, not opt-out
  • Privacy embedded into design — core requirement, not a bolt-on
  • Full functionality — privacy and usability are not zero-sum
  • End-to-end security — protect data throughout its lifecycle
  • Visibility and transparency — open for independent verification
  • Respect for user privacy — keep individual interests uppermost
3.5–3.6

Data sovereignty & technical toolkit

  • GDPR: "Right to be Forgotten" and "Data Portability."
  • Data Colonialism: data centers in developing nations harvest local data for Western AI models without local consent.
  • k-Anonymity: each individual indistinguishable from at least k-1 others in a dataset.
  • Differential Privacy: mathematical "noise" sharing group patterns without individual data.
  • Federated Learning: train AI on local devices — raw data never leaves the user's hardware.
FRT (Facial Recognition): Clearview AI scraped billions of photos without consent → banned in UK, Italy, Canada. Technical capability ≠ ethical legality.
4.1–4.2

The myth of the neutral algorithm

Objectivity Bias: the false belief that math = neutral. Software is a mirror — training on historical data trains on historical inequalities. GIGO.

  • Selection Bias: training data doesn't represent real-world population (medical AI from wealthy hospitals only).
  • Label Bias: "ground truth" is itself biased (arrest records ≠ crime — some neighborhoods are more heavily policed).
  • Optimization Bias: maximizing click-through rate without accounting for polarization as a social cost.
4.3 — Critical!

The impossibility of universal fairness

You cannot satisfy all fairness definitions simultaneously.

MetricMeaning
Demographic ParityEqual outcomes across groups (20% loans to Group A, 20% to Group B)
Individual FairnessSimilar people get similar outcomes
Predictive Rate ParityModel accuracy is equal for all groups
If base rates differ between groups → mathematically impossible to achieve both Demographic Parity and Predictive Rate Parity. You must make an ethical choice.
Case study — COMPAS

Recidivism prediction in U.S. courts

ProPublica found COMPAS was twice as likely to falsely flag Black defendants as "high risk." Developers argued it had Predictive Parity.

Key lesson: a model can be "accurate" in its predictions while being "systemically cruel" in its errors.
4.4–4.7

Proxies, mitigation & XAI

  • Algorithmic Redlining: remove "race" but model re-discovers it via Zip Code or shopping habits.
  • Pre-processing: re-weight training data for underrepresented groups.
  • In-processing: add fairness constraint to loss function during training.
  • Post-processing: adjust decision thresholds after model is built.
  • XAI — Local (SHAP/LIME): explains a single decision. SHAP assigns each feature an importance value.
  • XAI — Global: Feature Importance rankings across all data.
  • Explanation Trap: explanations can mask bias while appearing plausible (Faithfulness problem).
EU AI Act: High-Risk AI (hiring, credit, justice, healthcare) must allow human oversight and interpretability.
5.1–5.2

Who owns AI-generated code?

If an AI trains on 1B lines of public code and generates a function, who is the author? The data writer? The model trainer? The prompt user?

Fair Use (developer view)

Training is "transformative" — model learns patterns, not copies. Like a human reading a textbook.

Market Displacement (creator view)

AI reproduces style so perfectly the original creator is no longer hired. Not learning — economically cannibalizing.

2026 legal consensus: AI-generated content cannot be copyrighted alone. A "Human Spark" of significant creative input is required.
5.3

Open Source AI (OSI v1.0, 2025)

To be ethically "Open," a model must provide:

  • Open Weights — the parameters of the trained model
  • Open Training Code — the scripts used to build the model
  • Data Provenance report — for auditing bias and copyright (not raw data — that violates privacy)
5.4–5.5

Licensing & the GPL conflict

  • GPL (General Public License): "Copyleft" — any derivative work must also be open source.
  • Ethical Licenses: "The Software shall be used for Good, not Evil" — unenforceable because Good/Evil are legally undefined.
  • GitHub Copilot conflict: if Copilot suggests code 90% identical to a GPL project, your private repo may legally have to become open source.
  • Fix: Code Attribution Scanners alert you when AI suggestions match known repositories too closely.

6.1–6.2

From tools to agents — the Responsibility Gap

Computers were tools (hammer). AI systems that learn post-deployment are Agents. Emergent behaviors can arise that creators never programmed.

Responsibility Gap (Robert Sparrow): machine causes harm but it's unjust to blame the operator (who didn't decide) or the programmer (who couldn't predict emergent behavior). Solution: Meaningful Human Control (MHC) — not just a button; human must understand logic and have actual capability to override.
6.3–6.4

Autonomous vehicles & LAWS

  • Trolley Problem in AVs: 2026 consensus = Fairness over Favoritism. Minimize Overall Risk, not demographic judgments.
  • "Self-Sacrifice" Mode: some frameworks say AVs should prioritize pedestrians — occupants accepted risk by entering.
  • LAWS (Lethal Autonomous Weapon Systems): Martens Clause — "dictates of the public conscience" apply. Can AI distinguish a wounded soldier from an active combatant in fog of war? Current models struggle.

Automation Bias: humans trust the computer even when it's clearly wrong. Air France 447 — passive monitors for hours were psychologically unable to take over when systems failed.
7.1

The carbon cost of compute

  • Data centers in 2026: over 1,000 Terawatt-hours annually — rivaling entire nations like Japan.
  • Training a large model emits hundreds of metric tons of CO₂; inference multiplies that by orders of magnitude.
  • Water problem: millions of liters for cooling — competing with agriculture in water-stressed regions.
  • Green Coding: Python is often 75× more energy-intensive than C++ for the same task.
Ethical imperative: optimize for energy as strictly as you optimize for latency.
7.2–7.3

E-waste & supply chain

  • Global e-waste projected to exceed 60 million metric tons by 2026. Most exported to developing nations where informal recycling exposes workers to toxic lead and mercury.
  • Planned Obsolescence: designing software requiring the latest hardware — forcing users to discard functional devices.
  • Cobalt: 70%+ mined in DRC, linked to child labor. Under Social Contract Theory, the "painless" user experience should not be built on "painful" miner labor.
7.4–7.5

The digital divide 2.0

  • 2.2 billion people remain offline. 5G is standard in high-income countries.
  • AI Divide: "AI-poor" nations become data colonies — providing raw data for Western models without owning the resulting IP.
  • Linguistic Imperialism: English-centric LLMs risk erasing local dialects and cultural nuances.
